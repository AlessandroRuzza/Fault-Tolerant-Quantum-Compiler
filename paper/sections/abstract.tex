\begin{abstract}
Running a quantum circuit fault-tolerantly on a surface-code processor reduces
to two coupled tasks: placing logical qubits on a two-dimensional lattice and
routing the lattice-surgery operations that execute its gates. Unlike the near-term (NISQ) setting,
the bottleneck is not $\SWAP{}$ insertion but the routing corridors---free lanes
in the lattice through which the gates are routed. We
present a \emph{Gaussian potential-field} heuristic for the placement
sub-problem: each candidate tile in the lattice is scored by superposing repulsive fields that preserve routing space and attractive Gaussian fields that pull $T$-heavy qubits toward magic states and tightly interacting
qubits toward their partners. We implement the
method in an open-source C++20 OpenQASM compiler and evaluate it against a
randomized-restart baseline and the state-of-the-art WISQ compiler. Across
\todo{$N$} circuits the field placement reduces routing steps by \todo{$X\%$} on
average over the randomized baseline and by \todo{$Y\%$} over WISQ, while
compiling roughly \todo{$Z\times$} faster than WISQ's solver-based scheduler. 
The method also offers a tighter-packing mode that certifies
routability by connectivity instead of reserving the $3\times3$ patch spacing WISQ
requires, fitting circuits onto strictly smaller grids: this trades a modest
increase in routing steps for up to \todo{$G\%$} fewer physical qubits, at the
same low compilation cost.
\end{abstract}
